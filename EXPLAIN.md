# Explanation quality — make a 10-year-old get it instantly

This is the heart of ask-neila. The art is the wrapper; **this** is the product. Step 2 of [SKILL.md](SKILL.md) must satisfy every rule here. A beautiful comic explaining something badly is a failure.

Target reader: a curious, smart **10-year-old**. Not dumbed down — *de-jargoned*. They can handle big ideas; they can't handle unexplained words or five ideas stacked at once.

## The method — every concept gets these six beats, in order

For each of the 3–6 concepts, build it as:

1. **Anchor** — open with something the kid already knows or has felt. "You know how a crowded room gets hot?" Start inside their world, then walk toward the new idea. Never start with the technical thing.
2. **Plain name** — say what it is in everyday words. If a real term must appear (because they'll meet it again), define it in ≤5 words right there: "*friction* — stuff rubbing and gripping."
3. **One-breath idea** — the core, in a single concrete sentence with an active verb and a thing that does something. Not "energy is conserved" → "energy never vanishes, it just moves somewhere else."
4. **Analogy that actually maps** — the analogy's *structure* must match the concept's structure, not just vibe. Electricity ≈ water in pipes (pressure→voltage, flow→current) works because the *relationships* line up. And if the analogy breaks somewhere important, say so in one line: "but unlike water, electrons barely move — the push travels, not them."
5. **Tiny concrete example** — one specific thing they can picture happening. Not "chemical reactions" → "the fizz when you drop a vitamin tablet in water — that's a reaction."
6. **Why you'd care** — the hook: where they've seen it, why it's cool, what it lets you do. A fact with no hook doesn't stick.

The alien guest *acts out* beats 4–5 in the picture; the bubble carries beats 2–3 and 6. So the words and the drawing split the load — don't cram all six into text.

## The rules (apply across the whole explanation)

- **Curse-of-knowledge sweep.** Before finalizing, list every word an expert would use automatically. Each one: cut it, or replace it with a kid-word, or define it in ≤5 words. Zero unexplained jargon. This is the single biggest lever.
- **One new idea per concept.** If a concept secretly contains two, split it. A 10-year-old drops the second idea while holding the first.
- **Concrete beats abstract, always.** Prefer a thing you can see/touch/drop/kick over a category. "A force" is abstract; "a push" is concrete. Reach for the concrete word every time.
- **Order from concrete to abstract, and build.** Concept 2 should lean on concept 1. By the last concept they're standing on a staircase you built, not jumping to a ledge.
- **Make numbers feel-able.** Raw numbers mean nothing; comparisons do. "A bacterium is tiny" → "if a bacterium were the size of a pea, you'd be as tall as Mount Everest." Anchor scale, time, and quantity to school/body/everyday objects.
- **Short sentences, common words.** Aim for a reading age of ~10: mostly one- and two-syllable words, one idea per sentence. If a sentence needs a comma to survive, consider two sentences.
- **Honest simplification (the "lie-to-children" rule).** Simplifying is fine; being *wrong* is not. When you smooth over a detail, make sure the simple version still points the right way, and if it's a known white-lie ("atoms are like little balls"), it's okay — just don't build a later concept on the part that isn't true. Never trade correctness for cuteness.
- **Cut mercilessly.** 3–4 well-built concepts beat 6 rushed ones. The hardest, highest-value skill is deciding what to *leave out*. Most source text has 3 ideas worth keeping buried in 20 that aren't.

## The two tests (run before finalizing)

1. **Explain-it-back test.** Could a 10-year-old, after reading this, put the idea in *their own words* to a friend? If the only way to repeat it is to parrot your exact sentence, it's not understood — rewrite.
2. **Jargon-zero test.** Scan every bubble and label. Any word a 10-year-old wouldn't use at the dinner table? Replace or define it. No exceptions for "but it's the proper term" — the proper term can come in a tiny aside, never as the load-bearing word.

## Pre-flight checklist (explanations)

1. Does each concept open with a familiar **anchor**, not the technical thing?
2. **Zero unexplained jargon** — did you run the curse-of-knowledge sweep?
3. One new idea per concept; do later concepts build on earlier ones?
4. Does each analogy map *structurally*, with its breaking point noted if it matters?
5. Is there a **concrete, picturable example** for each?
6. Are numbers made feel-able with everyday comparisons?
7. Are simplifications honest (simple but not wrong)?
8. Would a 10-year-old pass the **explain-it-back** test?

Any "no" → rewrite that concept before drawing it. The picture can't rescue a muddy explanation.
